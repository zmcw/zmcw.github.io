The Surprising Utility of Romans 3:19

Pound for pound, Romans 3:19 is one of the richest veins in the entire scripture for the purpose of proving doctrinal arguments, and it states with dispositive authority what other scriptures might leave open to interpretation. It reads like this:

Now we know that whatever the law says, it says to those who are under the law, that every mouth may be stopped, and all the world may become guilty before God.

Romans 3:19, NKJV

In just these few short words, Paul makes no fewer than three strong assertions about the nature of our relationship to God and the Law. Their clarity, brevity, and unequivocality make them ideal building blocks for proving larger theories and resolving doctrinal contentions that arise on these subjects. Each one acts as a tool to discover deeper truths, and as a guardrail to keep us from erroneous understanding. It is worth exploring them in detail.

Paul frames the first tool he gives us as being natural, almost obvious: “Now we know that whatever the law says, it says to those who are under the law...” It’s easy to let this one slip by without fully grasping the weight of it. The contrapositive is actually so much more striking that we’ll prefer it over the original formulation, and it is this: if everything the Law says is directed at those who are under it, then (𝟙) the Law does not say anything whatsoever to those who are not under it. This, combined with the statement “you [believers] are not under law but under grace” from a few chapters later in Romans 6:14 gives us the corollary (𝟙.𝕒) that the law has nothing whatsoever to say to Christians.

This statement is quite controversial in some circles. Martin Luther, for example, famously asserted that the Law has three uses: one, as a “curb,” to prevent people from doing evil; two, as a “mirror,” to show us that we are sinners; and three, as a “guide,” to show Christians how to live [citation needed]. But this third use of the Law is disproved by (𝟙.𝕒), because it directly contradicts the notion that the Law does not speak to Christians. Should we be surprised? Yes! But it’s not completely untelegraphed.

Let us remember what the Law actually is (not): the Law is not a collection of moral principles like “do not steal”, “do not kill,” etc; rather, the law is these, bundled together with a consequence for breaking them. We know this from Romans 10:5, which says, “Moses writes this about the righteousness that is by the law: ‘The person who does these things will live by them.’” In other words, the righteousness of the Law is fundamentally of the form “do X and live, for some command X‍” (and by implication, “fail to do X and die”). The Law spells out its punishment for us in Ezekiel 18:20, “The soul who sins shall die,” echoed in Romans 6:23, “The wages of sin is death.” It is important to maintain this strict definition of Law, otherwise we end up thinking things like the law is repeated in the New Testament (it’s not; Ephesians 4:28’s “Let him who stole steal no more” lacks the Law’s consequent “or die,” and the same can be said about Christ’s “Go and sin no more” to the woman caught in the act of adultery and all the rest, so these by definition can’t be the Law), or that Jesus kept the Law (He didn’t; the Law is a shadow of Christ, and how can a man “keep” his shadow? rather, He fulfilled the Law, being that which the Law was modeled from, and much more), or some such thing, and the entire doctrine of the New Testament unravels for trying to fit two—Grace and Law—inside a sweater meant for one.

So that the Law has nothing to say to the Christian should make sense to anyone familiar with Romans 7:4, which reads, “So, my brothers and sisters, you also died to the law through the body of Christ...” Naturally, the law does not speak to the dead, among whom we number by faith. Furthermore, how could that which is not of faith (”and the law is not of faith,” Galatians 3:12) have anything to add to us, to whom “whatsoever is not of faith is sin” (Romans 14:23)?

The second tool Romans 3:19 equips us with concerns the purpose of the Law: “whatever the law says, it says ... that every mouth may be stopped, and all the world may become guilty before God.”

Furthermore, Paul writes in Galatians 3 that “the law was our schoolmaster to bring us unto Christ, that we might be justified by faith. But after that faith is come, we are no longer under a schoolmaster‍” (emph. added).

It ranks with the likes of “The just shall live by faith,” “the law entered that the offense might abound,” and “In Christ, neither circumcision [keeping the law] nor uncircumcision [breaking the law] availeth any thing, but faith, which worketh by love.”

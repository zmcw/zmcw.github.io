The Surprising Utility of Romans 3:19

Word for word, Romans 3:19 is one of the richest veins in the entire scripture for the purpose of proving doctrinal arguments, and it states with dispositive authority what other scriptures might leave open to interpretation. It reads like this:

Now we know that whatever the law says, it says to those who are under the law, that every mouth may be stopped, and all the world may become guilty before God.
Romans 3:19, NKJV

In just these few short words, Paul makes no fewer than three strong assertions about the nature of our relationship to God and the Law. This one verse compactly addresses these questions:

  1. To whom does the Law speak?
  2. What does the Law say to them?
  3. Why does the Law say what it says?

The answers grant us deep insights into Law, Sin, Grace, and the Gospel, and their clarity, brevity, and unequivocality make them ideal building blocks for proving larger theories and resolving doctrinal contentions that arise on these subjects. Each one acts as a tool to discover deeper truths, and as a guardrail to keep us from erroneous understanding. It is worth exploring them in detail.

Paul frames the first tool he gives us as being natural, almost obvious: “Now we know that whatever the law says, it says to those who are under the law...” And it does sound almost tautological in a way, so it’s easy to let this one slip by without fully grasping the weight of it. The contrapositive is actually so much more striking that we’ll prefer it over the original formulation, and it is this: if whatever the Law says is directed at those who are under it, then (𝟙) the Law does not say anything whatsoever to those who are not under it. This, combined with the claim that “you [believers] are not under law but under grace” from a few chapters later in Romans 6:14 gives us the corollary (𝟙.𝕒) that the law has nothing whatsoever to say to Christians.

This statement would be quite controversial in some circles, if anyone dared to make it. Martin Luther, for example, famously asserted that the Law has three uses: one, as a “curb,” to prevent people from doing evil, via the pangs of conscience; two, as a “mirror,” to show us that we are sinners; and three, as a “guide,” to show Christians how to live [citation needed]. But this third use of the Law is disproved by (𝟙.𝕒), because it directly contradicts the notion that the Law does not speak to Christians. Should we be surprised? Yes! It’s very natural to think that the Law should play some continuing role in our lives, because (and a whole essay could be written on this topic alone) the work of the Law is written on our hearts (Romans 2:15). We operate by default within its frame of reference⁠—⁠i.e. in the mode of making accusations, usually of others, and defenses, usually of ourselves or our friends⁠—⁠and we have to work hard to break free of that. But surprising though our theorem is, it’s not completely unforeshadowed by what information we already have.

Let’s remember what the Law actually is: the Law is not a collection of moral principles like “do not steal,” “do not kill,” etc. Rather, the Law is these, bundled together with a consequence for breaking them. We know this from Romans 10:5, which says, “Moses writes this about the righteousness that is by the law: ‘The person who does these things will live by them.’” In other words, the righteousness of the Law is fundamentally of the form “do X and live,” for some command X (and by implication, “fail to do X and die”). The Law spells out its punishment in Ezekiel 18:20, “The soul who sins shall die,” echoed in Romans 6:23, “The wages of sin is death.” This if-then structure is essential to the nature of any law; without it, what you have is a suggestion or recommendation or something, but not a law. And it’s important to keep this precise definition in mind, because otherwise we end up thinking things like the Law is repeated in the New Testament (it’s not; Ephesians 4:28’s “Let him who stole steal no more” lacks the Law’s consequent “or die,” and the same can be said about Christ’s “Go and sin no more” to the woman caught in the act of adultery, and all the rest, so these by definition can’t be the Law), or that Jesus kept the Law (He didn’t; the Law is a shadow of Christ, and how can a man “keep” his shadow? rather, He fulfilled the Law, being that which the Law was modeled from, and much more), or some such thing, and the entire doctrine of the New Testament unravels for trying to fit two⁠—⁠Grace and Law⁠—⁠inside the sweater meant for one.

So with the right definition in mind, it should make sense that the Law has nothing to say to Christians, as the very thing that makes the Law a “law,” namely the penalty of death, was already spent on Christ, is incapable of reaching us now. And the rest of the scripture confirms this wonderful⁠—⁠but yes, strange⁠—⁠state of affairs. For example, Paul writes, “Therefore, my brethren, you also have become dead to the law through the body of Christ [...] 6Now we are released from the law, having died to that which held us captive, so that we serve in the new way of the Spirit and not in the old way of the written code” (Romans 7:4, 6). Obviously, the Law does not speak to the dead; the dead are no longer under the Law’s juris-diction, which word comes from two Latin components literally meaning “law-speak.” Who ever heard of putting a dead man on trial? Furthermore, how could that which is not of faith (“and the law is not of faith,” Galatians 3:12) have anything to add to us, to whom “whatsoever is not of faith is sin” (Romans 14:23)? That’s worth saying again: whatsoever is not of faith is sin, and the Law⁠—⁠remember, “do thus and so for righteousness”⁠—⁠is not of faith. This doing of the Law is the very opposite of faith, because the opposite of faith is fact: “Faith is the evidence of things not seen” (Hebrews 11:1). So if you can see it (like you can see me doing the law), then it’s not “of faith.” The righteousness we have in Christ, on the other hand, is actually invisible because it’s in the future; for now, I still look like a sinner to an uninformed observer (c.f. the comforting words in 1 John 2:1, “My little children, I am writing these things to you so that you may not sin. But if anyone does sin, we have an advocate with the Father, Jesus Christ the righteous...”). Yet God calls those things which are not as though they were (Romans 4:17), and bids me do the same, right in the middle of my squalor. That’s the reason it can even be called “righteousness by faith.”

Galatians 5:4 says, “Christ is become of no effect unto you, whosoever of you are justified by the law; you are fallen from grace,” and the statement in (𝟙) is a readymade for defying the gravity of the Law. Any attempts to reintroduce elements of the Law⁠—⁠which attempts are often disguised to sound well-meaning and reasonable to our Law-based sensibilities, e.g. “Well, if so-and-so were really a Christian, then they wouldn’t have done that,” or the perennial favorite, “repent and turn from your sins, sister, and God will forgive you”⁠—⁠should be countered flatly with a recitation of (𝟙).

The second tool Romans 3:19 equips us with concerns the purpose of the Law: “whatever the law says, it says ... that every mouth may be stopped, and all the world may become guilty before God.” We knew that the Law only speaks to those under the Law, not to Christians. But this part informs us that, even when it’s speaking to non-Christians, (𝟚) whatever the Law says, it says it to condemn you before God. In other words, the Law has no other purpose than to bring its audience to muted guilt for their sins against it⁠—⁠no excuses, no defenses, just guilty silence.

This second axiom contradicts Martin Luther’s designation of the Law as a “curb” to restrain the evil in the world. But as if that weren’t enough, it turns out that the whole purpose of the Law from the very beginning was actually to make sin abound! “Now the law entered in, that sin might abound...” (first part of Romans 5:20). Far from being a bane to evil, the Law is actually the strength of sin! “The sting of death is sin, and the strength of sin is the law” (1 Corinthians 15:56). Paul describes the dire state of affairs in Romans 7:

7What then shall we say? That the law is sin? By no means! Yet if it had not been for the law, I would not have known sin. For I would not have known what it is to covet if the law had not said, “You shall not covet.”
8But sin, seizing an opportunity through the commandment, produced in me all kinds of covetousness. For apart from the law, sin lies dead.
9I was once alive apart from the law, but when the commandment came, sin came alive and I died.
10The very commandment that promised life proved to be death to me.
11For sin, seizing an opportunity through the commandment, deceived me and through it killed me.
Romans 7:7-11, ESV

The Law is not your friend: “for the law works wrath” (Romans 4:15). But this was the plan from the beginning, and in a strange and wonderful twist, God transformed what would have been our sure damnation into an integral part of the mechanism for our salvation: “...where sin abounded, grace did more abound” (second part of Romans 5:20). Someone might say that the Law doesn’t actually create the sin, it just shows you what’s already there. And that’s where Martin Luther and I agree. The Law does make a superb mirror to show you how wretched you are: “for by the Law is the knowledge of sin” (Romans 3:20). The mirror doesn’t create the dirt you see on your face, of course. But this doesn’t change the fact that the Law’s sole and simple purpose is to indict and convict you of the capital offense of being a sinner. Any restraint from evil this produces in you is an accident, and insofar as it fosters any sense of accomplishment or righteousness, it is actually contrary to the Law’s stated purpose: to crush all hope of keeping it, so that you can see the reality of your situation, and, possibly, start searching for righteousness outside of yourself; to finally wring out of you, after all your best attempts have failed, one last cry, one last desperate gasp for air: “O wretched man that I am! who shall deliver me from the body of this death?” (Romans 7:24). Dante wrote that above the gates of Hell the words are inscribed, “All hope abandon, ye who enter here.” But these words are perhaps just as appropriate for the entrance to the temple of the Law.

So the sequence is this: where the Law is strong, sin abounds. But where sin abounds, grace superabounds. And where grace superabounds, love abounds; for who is forgiven much loves much (Luke 7:47). This journey from Sin to Law to Grace to Love is the road that everyone Christian takes to Christ. Look at Paul: Pharisee of Pharisees, of the tribe of Benjamin, circumcised the eighth day, zealous persecutor of the Church, defender of the Law. But the harder he worked to keep the Law, the more material Sin had to use against him. This ended up compelling him to seek Grace, and he found it in Christ. For the critical role it plays in our salvation, namely, driving us to look for grace, Paul gave the Law a new name in Galatians 3:24-25: “the law was our schoolmaster to bring us unto Christ, that we might be justified by faith.” Then he adds, “But after that faith is come, we are no longer under a schoolmaster” (emph. added). When it comes to the Law, our only hope is to die, so that we can be raised in Christ.

Another enlightening result of this principle is: (𝟚.𝟙) if what you’re hearing isn’t condemning you, then it isn’t the Law. So when Jesus, as we mentioned before, told the woman caught in the act of adultery, “Neither do I condemn you; go and sin no more” (John 8:11), He can’t have been delivering the Law, as His words weren’t condemning her. Similarly, when the scripture says, “For God did not send His Son into the world to condemn the world, but that the world through Him might be saved” (John 3:17), it tells us that Jesus didn’t come to deliver the Law, because the Law only condemns. (It’s almost like Jesus said, “I didn’t come into the world to condemn the world,” and the Law says, “I came into the world to condemn the world!”)

Who does the law apply to? Everyone.

It ranks with the likes of “The just shall live by faith,” “the law entered that the offense might abound,” and “In Christ, neither circumcision [keeping the law] nor uncircumcision [breaking the law] availeth any thing, but faith, which worketh by love.”
